Should You Be Reading Yet?

26th April 2022

If you've already started learning hiragana and katakana, I honestly believe you could start right now. If you haven't realised it yet, you've already done all the heavy lifting at school and at home. There's enough transferable language knowledge in your head and all you need to do is to just test the water. Just know that if you're doing your learning outside of a framework like a school, you'll struggle way more to make things stick. However, if you can ride out the tough times without losing your interest, then carry on. If not, look for a language course or a reading group, or anything else you can find to get outside interaction (in fact, do that anyway). Then dedicate some time in your day to reading something small and make a habit of it. To close, I'll reiterate something I hear a lot, "Language learning isn't a race, it's a marathon; you need to pace yourself."
With that being said, here are some places you can start practicing your reading (at no charge).



This is a beginner's goldmine. You have access to the first two "episodes" of more than 20 series for free, and you don't even have to sign up for an account. It's accompanied by brilliant voice-acting and grammar is thoroughly explained and embellished on. The interface is dead-easy to use and can even hook into the Kanji learning platform WaniKani to match your current reading level. I highly recommend anyone with an interest in Japanese use this, no matter your stage of learning.



At this level you should probably know enough japanese to navigate a web interface, or at least know a plugin to help you with hard to read kanji. Here's where you're pretty lucky though... If at the very least you recognise the Kanji for 'read' in Japanese, you'll be enough of the way there to access the countless free content this website has to offer. It's hard to pass up on something like this. Just don't forget you're reading from right to left.



This is more for reading around specific vocabulary. Don't understand a word? Sure you can look it up in a dictionary and get the meaning at a glance, but this is a surface level understanding. At a later stage, you're going to get more from seeing words in context.

Kanji



Been around for ages. Spaced repitition. Heavy use of mnemonics to aid memory recall. Large and very helpful community. Meanings often limited, might want to look up in a dictionary 'jisho' 'weblio' so you can add meanings that should be there Made up radicals, radicals often differ from their native meaning No undo

Grammar



Plenty example sentences in the early stages. Natively voiced. Explanations of nuances. Undo if you make an oops
